Who?

Daniel Charms -- a.k.a. Daniil Kharms, Kaarms, Haarms -- achieved fame as an avant-garde eccentric in the '20s and '30s in Leningrad. In '27, Charms together with a number of experimental writers formed the literary group Oberiu, a "left flank" union between Futurist aesthetics and Formalist approaches. Their publicity stunts including a rooftop appearance by Charms caused minor sensation and they succeeded in presenting a highly unconventional theatrical evening. Among the Oberiu catch phrases were: 'Art is a cupboard' and 'Poems aren't pies; we aren't herring'.

In the Stalinist years the time for propagating experimental modern art had passed and tolerance for such frivolities was plummeting. Hostile journalistic attention ensured the hurried dispersing of the Oberiu group. Charms was arrested (accused of 'distracting the people from building of socialism by means of trans-sense verses') and exiled to Kursk. The exile was brief, the times being described as 'relatively vegetarian'. Nevertheless little work was to be done after.

The outbreak of war brought and end to everything: Charms was arrested in August '41 and possibly died of starvation in the prison hospital in February 1942. His work was 'rehabilitated' during the Khrushchev 'Thaw', but most of his adult writings had to await the Gorbachev period for publication in Russia.

Both starvation and arrest were anticipated in a number of Charms' writings. Hunger and poverty were constant companions; indeed, Charms can claim to be the poet of hunger.
